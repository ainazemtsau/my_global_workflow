     ## Central question

     Which playable gas interaction situations show that gas is the core gameplay system before cargo/containment continues?

     Plain version:

     > Когда игроки на floor спрашивают “что нам делать?”, ответ должен быть не “нести банку” и не “избегать туман”, а “считать газовое поле, использовать/изменять его, рискнуть value, защитить route/людей и решить, когда уходить”.

     A situation counts only if it translates into gameplay:

     - what players want;
     - what players see/read;
     - what players do;
     - what they risk;
     - what changes in world/player/cargo/route/gas state.

     It does not count if it is only an abstract label: reaction front, anomaly, pressure, cargo accident, rare state, hazard.

     ## Why now

     Cargo/containment is paused because it can swallow the gas system and turn the game into “collect, carry, sell”.

     The current risk is not that there are no gas topics. The risk is that “reaction”, “flow”, “source”, “anomaly”, “active condition”, “value” and “accident” stay as words instead of playable situations.

     This lab asks whether gas already has enough playable situation grammar to route into canon-forge before returning to cargo/containment.

     ## Plain-language current understanding

     Gas is the live board, not a pickup.

     Players enter a floor with a real but incomplete read. The read is not a fake lottery: if a condition is shown, it exists. The uncertainty is about exact source, scale, behavior, danger, usefulness, route relation and hidden extra factors.

     Inside the floor, players learn by contact: moving, watching gas move, trying bounded actions, seeing consequences, and revising the plan.

     Gas value is not raw volume. A visible gas can be collected, but the valuable thing is a state-window: clean, rare, deep, stable enough, useful-volatile, pushed, stabilized, readable, returnable, or procedure/readiness-relevant.

     Active condition is not loot by default. It is a world-cause: heat source, cold sink, pressure leak, radiation source, organics, contaminated bypass, broken scrubber, vent flow, compressor, sealed chamber, catalyst, material, route state.

     Cargo is downstream. Cargo matters when captured gas becomes custody and return liability. But cargo does not prove gas is core by itself.

     Anomaly is allowed as flagship gas expression, but not as secret monster AI. It may look alive; it must work as field/front/flow/pressure/density/temperature/reaction/condition consequence.

     Paper cannot certify fun. This lab can prepare a canon-forge and later grey-box route; it cannot claim fun is proven.

     ## Scenes / models explored

     These are not a fixed-count bundle. They are the useful paper situations that survive the lab because each one changes what players want, read, do, risk, and alter in the world.

     ### Model — Source / flow changes the floor while the team works

     Where are we?

     A service cluster with a visible leak, vent, jet, shaft flow, broken valve, cracked reservoir or persistent source. Gas is moving through the level, not sitting as a spawned static cloud.

     What do players want?

     They want to use the moving gas before it ruins the floor: capture a state-window, redirect it, isolate it, keep a route safe, or exploit the source to create better value.

     What do players read?

     They read where gas comes from, where it is going, whether density is rising, whether a front is approaching a route, whether pressure is building, and whether opening/closing paths changes flow.

     The read should come from world signs: visible movement, density, sound, pressure cues, vents, doors, gauges, condensation, turbulence, floor-level layer, or a front rolling toward an exit.

     What can players do?

     They can open/close a path, isolate a room, redirect a flow, extract from a stream, vent a dangerous buildup, hold a route, call a teammate back, or leave the source alone.

     Why is the greedy/risky option tempting?

     Keeping the source active can create more or better gas value. A stream can refill a pocket, push a state, feed a reaction, or create a richer sample.

     Why is the safe option costly?

     Venting, quenching or isolating may destroy value, reduce yield, close a route, spend time, or make the floor less useful for future work.

     What changes in the world/player/cargo/route/state?

     Gas density moves. A route becomes safe, worse, blocked, contaminated, pressurized or temporarily usable. A captured sample appears. A teammate may become occupied holding route/source/custody.

     What can go wrong?

     The source keeps filling the floor; a route back becomes bad; gas reaches another gas; a jet knocks cargo/player; an extraction takes too long; a teammate gets cut off by the flow the team created.

     What remains unresolved?

     Exact source types, controls, vent tools, solver details, timings and final VFX.

     ### Model — Active condition makes a gas state valuable and dangerous

     Where are we?

     A cold medical storage, hot compressor, pressure-heavy bypass, contaminated lab, radiation source room, organics cluster, cold sink, broken scrubber or catalyst-dust area.

     What do players want?

     They want to understand what the condition does to gas/value/route/cargo now. They may want to push a sample near it, avoid it, stabilize through it, or mark the procedure for a later run.

     What do players read?

     They read the condition as a world-cause: heat, cold, pressure, radiation signal, organic contamination, airflow, broken machinery, material trace, seal behavior, residue, instability, sample drift.

     If pre-entry read says the condition exists, it exists. The uncertainty is exact source, scale, strength, access, interaction and danger.

     What can players do?

     They can take a safe sample, move a sample closer to the condition, hold it there briefly, route around the condition, use a cold pocket to stabilize, avoid contamination, or abandon the push.

     Why is the greedy/risky option tempting?

     The condition can make a sample cleaner, deeper, rarer, more active, more useful-volatile, more readable, or more readiness-relevant.

     Why is the safe option costly?

     Avoiding or stabilizing early may preserve people/route but kill the higher ceiling. The team leaves with lower value or only knowledge/procedure.

     What changes in the world/player/cargo/route/state?

     Gas state changes; cargo becomes better/worse/less stable/more returnable; route can become contaminated or pressure-risky; a player becomes custody/condition holder; return liability increases.

     What can go wrong?

     The sample spoils, surges, contaminates, becomes hard to hold, forces release, worsens shared air, or requires a rescue while value is still unbanked.

     What remains unresolved?

     Exact condition roster, exact push outcomes, exact sample stability, scanner/intel model, container stats.

     ### Model — Reaction front is a moving decision, not a reaction table

     Where are we?

     Two gas states meet at a doorway, corridor edge, vent stream, shaft layer, broken containment site, or source-flow boundary.

     What do players want?

     They want to decide whether to use, quench, redirect, capture residue, stay clear, or let the reaction reshape route/value.

     What do players read?

     They read a boundary/front: color/density shift, sound, heat/cold cue, pressure pulse, residue, corrosion trace, moving edge, changing visibility, unstable line between gases.

     What can players do?

     They can close a door, open a vent, redirect one gas, quench the edge, capture a post-reaction residue, push a sample through the front, or retreat before the front reaches the return route.

     Why is the greedy/risky option tempting?

     The front can produce a valuable residue, transformed state, pressure charge, route opening, cleaner sample, or knowledge/procedure.

     Why is the safe option costly?

     Quenching or avoiding the front can remove the best value opportunity and may spend a cold pocket/tool/route option.

     What changes in the world/player/cargo/route/state?

     A front moves; gas transforms, quenches, flashes, contaminates, leaves residue, creates pressure, damages seals, or changes route safety. Players reposition and may split roles: one reads/calls, one controls route, one handles custody.

     What can go wrong?

     The reaction becomes a blast, pressure event, contamination wave, route closure, cargo leak trigger, or opaque random death if not readable.

     What remains unresolved?

     Exact finite reaction outcome grammar, gas identities/regimes, damage numbers, material implementation, final read/VFX.

     ### Model — Quench / stabilize / release is useful because it sacrifices something

     Where are we?

     A team has a valuable unstable state, a worsening route, a hot/pressure/cold condition, a moving front, or a teammate/cargo at risk.

     What do players want?

     They want to keep the run alive without flattening every risky decision into a free safety button.

     What do players read?

     They read instability and urgency: leaking state, rising density, route contamination, pressure buildup, cargo drift, teammate slowdown, front nearing exit, cold pocket available, quench path available.

     What can players do?

     Stabilize lower, quench a reaction, release a sample, vent cargo, abandon value, spend a route option, use a cold pocket, purge a corridor, or call retreat.

     Why is the greedy/risky option tempting?

     Holding the unstable state longer keeps higher value, deeper push potential, better output or better proof/knowledge.

     Why is the safe option costly?

     Safety reduces value, destroys rare regime, spends limited opportunity, ruins push potential, contaminates/uses route, or ends the floor early.

     What changes in the world/player/cargo/route/state?

     Value drops or becomes more returnable; danger reduces or moves elsewhere; route opens/closes; the team loses ceiling but gains return chance.

     What can go wrong?

     If release/quench is free, it dominates. If it is unreadable, it feels like punishment. If every situation has the same safety answer, gas loses depth.

     What remains unresolved?

     Exact tools, cooldowns, quantities, controls, stabilization outcomes, balance.

     ### Model — Anomalous/flagship gas creates rescue without becoming creature AI

     Where are we?

     A deeper or rare condition creates a dense anomalous front: tendrils, hands-like shapes, pulling layer, pressure-grip, breathing cold bank, flock-like fog, or cargo-snatching flow.

     What do players want?

     They want fear, curiosity and value without breaking the gas-system lock. They may want to cross, observe, capture trace, rescue a teammate, recover cargo, or mark the condition for future readiness.

     What do players read?

     They read field behavior, not intent: flow direction, pressure gradient, density, cold/heat relation, front edge, tether-like gas strands, route closure, pull direction, cargo drag, affected area.

     What can players do?

     Stay out of active zone, redirect flow, break line/pressure, quench edge, pull teammate, release cargo, mark safe timing, or abandon value.

     Why is the greedy/risky option tempting?

     The anomaly may produce rare/deep value, procedure, route proof, strong emotional memory, or readiness for the next depth.

     Why is the safe option costly?

     Avoiding it may mean leaving the best discovery/value/procedure behind and not learning the flagship behavior.

     What changes in the world/player/cargo/route/state?

     A player is slowed, pulled, separated, forced to release, or needs rescue. Cargo may be dropped or moved. Route may be closed/opened by field behavior. Team roles appear naturally.

     What can go wrong?

     It becomes monster AI: target choice, pursuit, pathfinding, intent, player-hunting. That violates parent locks unless explicitly reopened.

     What remains unresolved?

     Exact anomaly forms, visuals, thresholds, player consequence rules, rescue/downed model, proof scene.

     ### Bridge model — Cargo leak as world accident, not cargo-centered design

     Where are we?

     The team is returning with a pushed unstable state, and the captured gas leaks or breaks into an external gas/flow/condition.

     What do players want?

     They want to save people, route and remaining value before safe return.

     What do players read?

     They read a leak, pressure hiss, contamination trace, casing stress, cargo instability, external gas front, reaction edge, or route consequence.

     What can players do?

     Stabilize, release, quench, separate from external gas, abandon cargo, rescue carrier, reroute, or accept a lower-value return.

     Why is the greedy/risky option tempting?

     Holding the cargo preserves the best value.

     Why is the safe option costly?

     Releasing or stabilizing may destroy the prize.

     What changes?

     World changes: leak, contamination, blast, quench, pressure, corrosion, route damage, worse air, rescue moment.

     What can go wrong?

     If this becomes the main design, gas collapses back into container management.

     What remains unresolved?

     Exact containment, leak rates, container stats, carry controls, accident tables.
