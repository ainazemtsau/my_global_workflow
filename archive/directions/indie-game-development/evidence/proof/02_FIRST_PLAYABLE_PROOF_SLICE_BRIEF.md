## 1. One-sentence player promise

A small co-op team commits to a risky lift descent to recover unstable value from a hostile gas/topology pocket, then must decide whether to push, reroute, secure partial value, retreat, or abandon the vessel before the lift locks the result.

This slice is intended to test Expedition as a connected co-op judgment loop, not as a gas simulation, loose sandbox, generic extraction run, or implementation plan.

## 2. Candidate playable slice loop

Working title: **Unstable Vessel at the Return-Path Split**.

```text
commit small abstract risk/tool roles at lift
→ descend into unknown route pocket
→ read forked route + gas pressure + dangerous vessel-value
→ spend one intervention to create temporary safety or access
→ attempt to stabilize/recover the unstable vessel
→ gas/topology/vessel consequence changes return conditions
→ team chooses push / reroute / secure / retreat / abandon
→ lift ascent locks result into consequence ledger
→ ledger creates a concrete reason for the next descent
```

The slice is one bounded expedition run. It is not a prototype scene specification and does not define production mechanics.

## 3. Mapping to accepted minimum Expedition loop

| Accepted minimum Expedition loop element | Candidate playable slice equivalent |
| --- | --- |
| Prepare risk / tools | The team chooses a small abstract pre-descent commitment: limited intervention roles/tokens, not exact tool stats. |
| Descend through lift | The lift is the entry threshold, commitment boundary, and later result lock-in point. |
| Read unknown route + gas + dangerous value | The team exits into a compact route fork with visible gas pressure and one unstable vessel-value pocket. |
| Spend tool / change route / create temporary safety | The team spends one abstract intervention to open access, create temporary safety, delay gas pressure, or stabilize the vessel. |
| Recover or stabilize dangerous vessel-value | The team attempts to secure, move, stabilize, or partially recover the unstable vessel-value marker. |
| Readable gas/topology/vessel consequence happens | The route or return path changes: gas pressure shifts, a path becomes unsafe, the vessel worsens, or the intervention creates a new tradeoff. |
| Choose push / reroute / secure / retreat / abandon | The team debates a costly choice before the lift locks the outcome. |
| Result is locked into consequence ledger | The lift ascent records extracted value, abandoned value, damaged vessel, spent/lost intervention, shifted gas, or route knowledge. |
| There is a reason for next descent | The ledger leaves a concrete state-based pull: abandoned vessel, mapped route, worsening leak, opened access, or lost intervention. |

## 4. Minimal player situation

Players are a 2-4 person expedition team.

The low-fidelity play area contains:

- **Lift room**: entry/exit threshold and result lock-in.
- **Short path**: faster access to the vessel but vulnerable to gas pressure on return.
- **Long path / service loop**: slower path that may become safer only if the team spends or preserves an intervention.
- **Vessel pocket**: contains one unstable value marker that is both reward and threat.
- **Return-path split**: after delay or intervention, the safe-looking return route may become unsafe.

The run has risk, stake, retreat, partial success, loss, and possible abandoned value. It should be understandable as an expedition without the facilitator explaining a lore premise.

## 5. What players do by hand

The playable proof can be run with paper, tokens, and a small route sketch.

Concrete hand actions:

1. Draw or place the lift, route fork, vessel pocket, gas pressure marker, and ledger area.
2. Choose a small pre-descent commitment: abstract intervention roles or tokens.
3. Descend by lift and reveal the route/gas/vessel situation.
4. Move team markers through the route sketch.
5. Place, advance, or shift gas pressure markers.
6. Spend one intervention token to create temporary safety/access, delay pressure, or stabilize the vessel.
7. Mark vessel state as stable, unstable, damaged, leaking, secured, or abandoned at a rough proof level.
8. Debate push / reroute / secure / retreat / abandon.
9. Ascend by lift and record the ledger result.

These are proof actions, not final production controls.

## 6. Lift / descent boundary

The lift does three jobs:

- **Entry threshold**: the run begins when the team descends and cannot freely undo commitment.
- **Exit threshold**: the team must return to lock any result.
- **Result lock-in**: after ascent, the ledger records what happened.

The lift is not yet a full lift-failure, contamination, capacity, timing, or UI design. Those mechanics remain unresolved.

## 7. Route / gas / topology pressure

Gas and topology are valid only when they change Expedition judgment.

In this slice, gas/topology pressure should affect at least two of:

- route choice;
- temporary safety/access;
- vessel risk;
- co-op coordination;
- retreat timing;
- consequence ledger;
- next-descent reason.

Minimal pressure example:

- the short path gives fast vessel access but becomes risky if gas shifts;
- the long path is safer but costs time or intervention;
- intervention can temporarily vent, seal, brace, open, or delay pressure without specifying exact gas chemistry;
- a delayed return may force the team to reroute, abandon value, or accept damaged value.

Gas is not proof by itself. Gas proves something only if it affects route, safety, vessel, co-op, judgment, or ledger.

## 8. Dangerous vessel-value situation

The vessel-value is a reward-threat object.

It is valuable because recovering it can create a positive ledger result.

It is dangerous because mishandling, delay, route pressure, or gas exposure can:

- damage it;
- reduce its value;
- make it leak;
- worsen route pressure;
- force carrying/stabilizing coordination;
- make abandonment rational.

The brief does not decide exact cargo slots, carrying models, value scoring, leakage formulas, or damage rules.

## 9. Co-op shared-state consequence

Co-op must change available decisions.

Shared-state elements:

- one route and return path;
- one gas/topology situation;
- one vessel-value state;
- limited shared interventions;
- shared retreat/extraction boundary;
- one consequence ledger result.

Removing one player, role, or shared resource should meaningfully change options. If a solo player can solve the situation while others perform parallel or decorative actions, this slice fails co-op proof.

Minimal co-op pressure examples:

- one player tracks or holds the return-path risk while another stabilizes vessel state;
- the team must choose whether an intervention protects people, route, or vessel;
- retreat requires coordinating vessel state and route state, not merely pressing an exit option.

## 10. Intervention and temporary safety / access

The slice uses one abstract intervention, not an exact tool roster.

Acceptable intervention meanings:

- create a temporary safe window;
- open or preserve access;
- delay gas pressure;
- stabilize the vessel;
- make the long path safer;
- make extraction possible but costlier.

The intervention should be costly enough that spending it early creates later pressure. It must not become a finalized tool, exact stat, recipe, or implementation task.

## 11. Judgment point

Main judgment point:

> The vessel is reachable or partly stabilized, but the return path is worse, the team has spent or risks losing its intervention, and the lift will lock the outcome. Do they push for fuller recovery, reroute through the slower path, secure partial value, retreat now, or abandon the vessel for a future descent?

The judgment point passes only if at least two options are plausibly defensible.

It fails if:

- retreat is always obviously right;
- pushing is always obviously right;
- the vessel is ordinary loot;
- gas is only a timer;
- co-op does not change the decision;
- the ledger result creates no next-descent reason.

## 12. Run ending

Possible run endings:

- **Clean success**: vessel extracted with acceptable damage/risk.
- **Partial success**: damaged vessel, partial value, or route cost accepted.
- **Loss**: intervention spent, no value recovered, route/vessel state worsened.
- **Abandoned value**: vessel left behind but now mapped, stabilized, damaged, leaking, or made more urgent.
- **Knowledge-only result**: no value extracted, but route/gas/vessel information creates a concrete next descent.

These endings are proof states, not economy/progression outcomes.

## 13. Consequence ledger result

The ledger records stateful consequences without designing economy, upgrades, base, rewards, or progression.

Possible ledger entries:

- vessel extracted;
- vessel damaged;
- vessel abandoned;
- vessel leaking or becoming more dangerous;
- route opened, blocked, or partially mapped;
- gas pressure shifted;
- intervention spent, lost, or preserved;
- team learned that the short path is unsafe under pressure;
- team learned a safer return route;
- next descent target identified.

Concrete next-descent reasons:

- recover abandoned unstable vessel before it worsens;
- use newly gained route knowledge;
- return through opened access;
- repair or replace a lost intervention;
- exploit a safer path learned during retreat;
- resolve a leak or route hazard created by the previous run.

## 15. Paper-run protocol / desk-run summary

No live player paper-run is claimed by this F0 artifact.

This brief defines a paper-run-ready protocol and a desk-run stress summary for R1 review.

Minimum materials:

- simple route sketch with lift, short path, long path, vessel pocket, and return split;
- gas/pressure markers;
- one unstable vessel-value marker;
- one or two abstract intervention tokens;
- ledger note area;
- run log.

Minimum roles:

- facilitator exposes only what players would plausibly read;
- player team of 2-4 people, or simulated roles if no live players are available;
- depressor/skeptic watches for false positives;
- ledger keeper records decisions and next-descent reason.

Minimum three low-fidelity runs:

1. **Baseline run**: does the situation read as an expedition without author rescue?
2. **Route / return pressure run**: does gas/topology change route, safety, return path, or decision timing?
3. **Vessel destabilization run**: does dangerous value create stake, loss, leakage, carrying/stabilizing pressure, or a reason to abandon?

Record after each run:

- when the team first discussed risk;
- when the team first discussed retreat;
- what made co-op necessary;
- what decision felt most painful;
- what was locked into the ledger;
- why the team would or would not descend again;
- what hidden rule the facilitator had to invent.

Desk-run stress summary:

- The candidate appears paper-runnable because it needs a route sketch, markers, one vessel, and abstract interventions.
- The candidate is weak if it needs exact carrying, death, gas chemistry, tool stats, inventory, or economy rules to be judged.
- The candidate fails if facilitator explanation is the only thing making the vessel valuable, retreat painful, or co-op necessary.

## 16. Follow-the-fun hypotheses

Minimum hypotheses:

- Players start speaking as an expedition team before the first intervention.
- The first tense discussion happens before lift result lock-in.
- Removing one player, role, shared intervention, or shared route state meaningfully changes options.
- Gas/topology changes at least one route, safety, retreat, vessel, or ledger decision.
- The dangerous vessel-value makes success feel risky, not merely rewarding.
- Retreat or abandon becomes reasonable and emotionally costly.
- The ledger creates a concrete state-based reason for the next descent.

Mark **false-positive fun** if the strongest enjoyment comes only from:

- watching gas move;
- breaking or manipulating the environment;
- experimenting with a route toy;
- optimizing generic loot extraction;
- enjoying a sandbox with no Expedition judgment.

False-positive fun does not prove this slice. It is a revise/kill signal for the candidate.

## 17. Red-team / depressor answers

| Depressor question | Answer for this candidate |
| --- | --- |
| If gas is replaced by a generic timer, does the slice stay almost the same? | It must not. Gas must alter route, safety window, vessel risk, co-op timing, or ledger. If it only counts down, the slice fails. |
| If dangerous vessel-value is replaced by ordinary loot, does tension stay almost the same? | It must not. The vessel must be reward-threat coupled. Ordinary loot would collapse the slice into generic extraction. |
| If co-op is replaced by parallel solo tasks, does anything essential disappear? | It should. Shared route, intervention timing, vessel state, retreat coordination, and ledger ownership should disappear or weaken. |
| If retreat is always obviously right or obviously wrong, is there real judgment? | No. At least two options must be plausibly defensible. |
| If the ledger leaves no concrete reason for next descent, what did the run prove? | Not enough. The run becomes a one-off scenario rather than Expedition loop proof. |
| If the strongest memory is "cool system" rather than "hard expedition decision," did it pass the right test? | No. System spectacle is supporting evidence only. |
| If exact cargo/carrying/death/lift/procgen/tool/gas rules are needed to judge it, is the slice too expensive or undefined? | Yes. Route to decision/revision instead of silently solving those mechanics. |
| If the slice works only because the facilitator explains stakes verbally, are the stakes in the play? | No. Stakes must appear through route, gas, vessel, intervention, retreat, and ledger states. |
| If one player can solve the situation while others assist decoratively, is co-op proof fake? | Yes. Co-op must change decisions, not just execution speed. |
| If no one would want another descent after the ledger result, where is the replay pull? | Missing. The slice must revise until the ledger creates a state-based pull. |

## 20. Gas-only rejection check

Gas-only proof is explicitly rejected.

The slice fails if it can pass by showing:

- gas movement;
- gas visuals;
- gas reactions;
- gas/grid technical competence;
- a timer-like pressure zone;
- a visually interesting simulation.

Gas is valid only when it changes at least two of:

- route choice;
- temporary safety/access;
- dangerous vessel-value risk;
- co-op coordination;
- retreat timing;
- push/reroute/secure/retreat/abandon judgment;
- consequence ledger;
- next-descent reason.

For this candidate, gas must be evaluated as Expedition pressure, not simulation quality.

## 21. Gas / grid / topology / destructibility relevance statement

Gas/topology relevance:

- The route split makes gas readable at route and return-path scale.
- Gas pressure can make the short path risky after the vessel is reached.
- Topology makes reroute/retreat meaningful because the long path may be safer but costlier.
- Gas can threaten vessel stability or value state.
- Shared gas/route state creates co-op coordination pressure.
- Ledger consequences can preserve shifted gas, blocked route, opened path, or abandoned vessel state.

Grid/destructibility relevance:

- Destructibility is not required for this slice.
- If destructibility is added later, it must change access, safety, return path, vessel risk, or decision cost.
- "It is cool that things break" is not proof.

Removal test:

- Remove gas/topology. If the team makes the same decision, the slice is weak or failed.

Swap test:

- Replace gas with a generic timer. If the play stays almost the same, the slice fails gas-only rejection.

Connection test:

- Gas/topology should connect to route, safety, vessel, co-op, retreat, and ledger, not sit in one isolated moment.

Spectacle trap test:

- If spectacle is stronger than judgment, revise or kill.

## 22. Cost-to-proof check

Cheapest honest proof form:

- paper route sketch;
- gas/pressure markers;
- one unstable vessel-value marker;
- one or two abstract intervention tokens;
- simple lift boundary;
- consequence ledger;
- 2-4 player roles or simulated roles;
- run log and depressor questions.

Acceptable proof cost:

- low-fidelity paper-run;
- no exact gas taxonomy;
- no exact reaction table;
- no exact tool catalog;
- no detailed carrying/inventory model;
- no death/downing/group-failure rules;
- no procedural generation logic;
- no economy/progression/base design;
- no implementation before identity can be judged.

Warning signs:

- the slice needs full gas chemistry to be interesting;
- the slice needs exact cargo or inventory to create stakes;
- the slice needs a digital prototype before retreat can be judged;
- the slice needs production UI/readability before any decision can be observed;
- the slice needs economy/progression to make value matter.

If any warning sign becomes blocking, stop and route to a decision/revision stage.
