## Executive conclusion

The research result is strong enough to move from research to a global operating plan. The default strategy should be:

**moderate calorie deficit + high protein + whole-food Mediterranean/DASH-like structure + three anchor meals + one controlled morning anti-grazing slot + low-friction tracking + modular meal prep.**

The strategy should not start with ketogenic dieting, alternate-day fasting, 5:2 fasting, extended fasting, or a strict no-snack rule. These options either do not add enough benefit over a well-controlled energy deficit, create higher adherence risk for this profile, or raise avoidable safety/lean-mass concerns.

The core user-specific problem is not simple hunger. The stronger model is:

**recent smoking cessation → oral/chewing drive and reward seeking → post-breakfast grazing → high-calorie snacks/sweets/bread/chips → energy surplus despite training history.**

Therefore the nutrition strategy must control not only calories/macros, but also timing, food environment, trigger visibility, and the post-breakfast decision loop.

## User-specific implications

1. **Fat loss is the priority; recomposition is a secondary benefit.** At 182 cm and 125 kg, the first clinically meaningful objective is not final ideal weight but the first 5–10% reduction, approximately 6–12.5 kg.

2. **Lean mass protection is central.** The user has a meaningful strength-training history and likely substantial muscle mass. The plan should preserve the ability to return to lifting, not simply maximize scale loss.

3. **The breakfast-to-lunch window is the critical control window.** The plan should actively engineer this period rather than assume willpower will solve it.

4. **Strict “no snack ever” is not the default.** For this profile, a controlled anti-grazing slot is more robust than an absolutist no-snack rule that can break into repeated unplanned decisions.

5. **Household sweets are an environmental variable, not a character issue.** The wife bringing sweets means the plan needs storage rules, visibility rules, and planned-treat rules.

6. **Tracking should be light but real.** The first two weeks need approximate calories and more reliable protein tracking to calibrate maintenance assumptions. After calibration, the burden can be reduced.

7. **Medical uncertainty matters.** Possible insulin resistance, unknown waist, no labs, and snoring make aggressive fasting, very-low-calorie dieting, and blind keto poor defaults.

## Evidence-by-topic synthesis

### Energy deficit

A moderate deficit is the best default. The research supports a working calibration range of **2400–2600 kcal/day** during the temporary no-strength-training phase, but this must be treated as a hypothesis, not a final truth. The true maintenance estimate must be validated against weekly average weight.

Operational interpretation:

- start with 2400–2600 kcal/day for 14 days;
- evaluate only by 7-day average weight, hunger, adherence, and morning grazing frequency;
- do not react to one-day scale noise;
- revise by 150–200 kcal only after enough data.

### Protein and lean mass

The strongest default target is **170–200 g protein/day**, with **~180 g/day** as the first operating target. Distribution should be 3–4 protein feedings, usually **35–55 g protein** each.

The plan should not calculate protein from 125 kg actual body weight without adjustment, because that can overstate the target. The target is a practical synthesis from obesity and sports-nutrition evidence.

### Training interaction

During the 1–2 week strength pause, protein remains high. Carbs do not need to be maximized. When lifting returns, calories do not automatically increase; the first adjustment is targeted carbohydrate around training if performance drops.

Training return rule:

- hold baseline calories for 1–2 weeks after lifting resumes;
- if performance, recovery, or soreness becomes limiting, add **100–250 kcal on training days**, mostly carbs around training;
- keep protein constant.

### Carbohydrates

Moderate carbohydrate intake is default. Low-carb but not keto is a conditional tool if it clearly reduces cravings and simplifies adherence. Keto is not a default due to burden, lipid uncertainty, and no clear long-term advantage for this profile.

Carbs should be structured, not demonized:

- breakfast carbs should be portioned and paired with protein/fiber;
- no sweet liquid/soft trigger foods in the morning;
- training-day carbs can be moved around workouts after strength training returns.

### Fats

Fats should be moderate, not extremely low and not ad-lib high-fat. The practical range for the first phase is **60–80 g/day**. Higher-fat foods should be portioned because they are easy to overshoot.

### Fiber, food volume, and ultra-processed foods

The plan should target **30–40 g fiber/day** once tolerated. Volume should come from vegetables, berries, legumes when practical, potatoes, whole grains, and lean protein sides. Ultra-processed snack foods are high-risk because they are easy to eat beyond hunger and directly match the user’s automatic-eating pattern.

### Meal timing and frequency

The default structure is:

- **3 anchor meals**;
- **1 planned morning anti-grazing slot**;
- dinner usually present but lighter; not zero by default.

This is superior to unrestricted grazing, and likely better than strict breakfast skipping because the user already has an established early schedule and post-breakfast vulnerability.

### Breakfast

Breakfast must be engineered as an anti-grazing meal, not just a first meal.

Default breakfast principles:

- 40–55 g protein;
- 10+ g fiber where feasible;
- low sweetness intensity;
- no pastry/chocolate/cookie/cereal-style trigger;
- enough volume that the next 2–3 hours are behaviorally stable.

### Fasting / time-restricted eating

Intermittent fasting can be used only if it reduces friction. It is not the default. Alternate-day fasting, 5:2, and extended fasting are poor starting options.

Decision:

- 14-day calibration: no fasting experiment;
- after calibration: possible mild time boundary if useful, such as no food after planned dinner;
- extended fasting: medical-only / avoid as lifestyle default.

### Diet breaks and refeeds

Diet breaks are useful if adherence, mood, training performance, or stress deteriorate. They are not mandatory from week 1. Refeeds are not a default and should not become a sanctioned binge.

### Hydration

Default target: **3.0–3.5 L/day fluid**, unless medical contraindications appear. This fits the user’s previous habit of ~3.5 L/day and is above the basic adult male adequate-intake reference, but still practical given body size, coffee, activity, and early routine.

### Caffeine

Default: 3 cups coffee/day can stay, but timing matters because bedtime is ~19:30–20:00. Last full coffee should usually be before noon, preferably earlier if sleep quality worsens.

### Smoking cessation and oral craving

The plan must include a non-food and low-calorie oral-substitution toolkit:

- sugar-free gum;
- mint;
- water / sparkling water / zero drink;
- tea;
- crunchy low-calorie vegetables;
- tooth brushing after breakfast;
- a rule that “mouth wants stimulation” is not automatically “body needs calories.”

### Household sweets and problem foods

The right policy is not “all sweets impossible forever” and not “free access.” The default is **controlled inclusion with environmental friction**:

- spouse sweets: not on visible surfaces;
- user sweets: only planned, pre-portioned, after a protein-containing meal;
- no sweets with coffee between tasks;
- no sweets in the post-breakfast grazing window during calibration.

### Meal prep

The plan should use modular prep rather than rigid 5-day meal prep:

- protein bases for 2–3 days;
- vacuum/freezer buffer for later days;
- vegetables partly fresh, partly frozen/zero-friction;
- daily assembly rather than fully prebuilt every meal;
- fallback meals defined before the week starts.

## What to use now

1. 14-day calibration at 2400–2600 kcal/day.
2. Protein target ~180 g/day; acceptable range 170–200 g/day.
3. Fat 60–80 g/day.
4. Fiber 30–40 g/day once tolerated.
5. Three anchor meals.
6. One planned anti-grazing slot between breakfast and lunch.
7. No morning sweets during calibration.
8. Sweets only planned, portioned, after a main meal with protein.
9. Daily morning weight, interpreted only as weekly average.
10. Weekly waist measurement.
11. Track food in the weekly Tracking Chat: approximate calories, protein, water, morning grazing, adherence, and problem-food events.

## What to avoid by default

- ketogenic diet as the main plan;
- alternate-day fasting;
- 5:2 fasting as a starter;
- extended fasting;
- unplanned sweets with coffee;
- unrestricted snack foods at home/workstation;
- skipping dinner after an oversized lunch as the default correction strategy;
- heavy macro obsession beyond the calibration period;
- turning refeeds/cheat meals into open-ended overeating.

## Conditional tests

After the first 14 days, test only one variable at a time:

1. If morning grazing remains high: change breakfast structure before reducing calories.
2. If adherence is good but weight loss is too slow: reduce 150–200 kcal/day or tighten sweets/environment rules.
3. If hunger is too high: add food volume/fiber or shift calories earlier before lowering calories.
4. If strength training returns and performance drops: add 100–250 kcal on training days from carbs around training.
5. If cognitive energy drops: inspect sleep, hydration, breakfast composition, and caffeine timing before changing the whole diet.
6. If planned sweets trigger overeating: suspend planned sweets for 7–14 days and replace with non-triggering sweet-like protein options.

## Safety constraints

This is not medical treatment. The plan should recommend clinician/lab follow-up for:

- blood pressure;
- waist circumference and waist-to-height ratio;
- fasting glucose and/or HbA1c;
- lipid profile;
- ALT/AST as clinically appropriate;
- snoring / possible sleep apnea screening;
- symptoms such as chest pain, severe dizziness, marked fatigue, fainting, severe sleep disruption, or abnormal shortness of breath.

Aggressive fasting, very-low-calorie dieting, and electrolyte manipulation are not default options.

## Decision rules

### Weight-loss speed

Use 7-day average weight.

- If loss is **0.4–0.8% body weight/week** and adherence/energy are acceptable: continue.
- If loss is **<0.3–0.4%/week for 2 weeks** and adherence is high: reduce 150–200 kcal/day or tighten problem-food environment.
- If loss is **>1.0%/week for 2 weeks** or hunger/fatigue is high: add 100–200 kcal/day or reduce diet rigidity.
- If weight is flat but adherence is low: fix adherence/environment first; do not cut calories immediately.

### Morning grazing

- If grazing occurs after breakfast 2+ days/week: inspect breakfast protein, fiber, sweetness, and the anti-grazing slot.
- If grazing is driven by mouth stimulation rather than hunger: use oral toolkit first.
- If grazing is true hunger: use planned protein snack.

### Sweets

- Planned sweet allowed only after main meal with protein.
- No sweet during post-breakfast-to-lunch window in calibration.
- If unplanned sweet occurs: log it, return to next planned meal, do not punish-compensate.

### Training return

- Keep nutrition baseline for 1–2 weeks after lifting resumes.
- If training drops: add carbs around training first, not random daily calories.
- If soreness/recovery is poor: check sleep, protein, calories, hydration, training load.

### Diet break

Use 7–14 days at estimated maintenance if:

- adherence deteriorates for 2+ weeks despite simplification;
- training performance falls materially after strength return;
- sleep or stress makes deficit unsustainable;
- weight has dropped substantially and hunger becomes persistently high.
